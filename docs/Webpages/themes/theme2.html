Race and Recognition

Much like today’s military system, the Civil War military depended on paperwork. Every soldier generated records: enlistment forms, muster rolls, payrolls, hospital registers, prisoner lists, pension files, and more. These documents were meant to track soldiers, pay them, and record for their service. But beyond that, these records also helped define race and belonging.

Historians increasingly argue that the military bureaucracy of the nineteenth century contributed to the production of racial categories. While these systems were at first designed to organize soldiers, it also required officials to classify individuals according to race, nationality, and legal status. In doing so, the army helped turn fluid identities into fixed administrative labels. Many recruits, however, did not fit neatly into the fixed classifications the army expected. Immigrants, Indigenous soldiers, and other minority groups complicated these categories. Officials had to decide how to record names, describe physical features, and determine where soldiers fit within the army’s racial hierarchy.

But these decisions were not without consequences. Racial classification shaped much of the soldier’s experience– where they were assigned, how they were paid, promotion eligibility, and how their service would be remembered. Paperwork and administrative procedure therefore became a strong tool for organizing recognition.


Archival Limitations and Struggles

Names were frequently misspelled or anglicized. Unfamiliar with foreign spellings, clerks often wrote them down phonetically or used simplified alternatives. It made their jobs easier, but as a result, one individual could appear under multiple spellings across different documents. For example, “John Achoo” could also be “John Afoo”. Other records attempted to place soldiers into broad racial categories that did not accurately reflect their identities. Historian Angela He argues that military bureaucracies often functioned as a “racial laboratory,” forcing individuals into predetermined categories that shaped how race itself was understood.

These records also determined how soldiers could later claim recognition. After the war, veterans relied on military paperwork to prove their service when applying for pensions or benefits. How accessible these records were determined whether former soldiers received support from the federal government. For marginalized soldiers, this process could be equated to a nightmare. Missing files, inconsistent names, and racial prejudice made it much harder to establish eligibility.

As Historians 

Today, historians rely on these same documents to reconstruct the lives of Civil War soldiers, but the records themselves reflect the biases and limitations of the institutions that created them. Knowing this requires scholars to read the archive in a specific way.

Military paperwork, for example, could not be read as neutral evidence, but rather part of the process through which race, citizenship, and belonging were defined during the Civil War. This period saw the formation of the nation’s first draft, and with that, the first rudimentary system for classifying individuals for military service. For Chinese soldiers, they were placed into the existing categories of “white,” “black,” or “mulatto,” purely at the discretion of the enlisting officer or enumerator.  Beyond preserving history, the archive also reveals how the state attempted to organize and interpret human difference. Ruthanne Lum McCunn notes that racial classifications varied geographically– one state might have classified Chinese men as “mulatto” while another might have identified them as “white.” These inconsistencies in how Chinese immigrants were categorized reveals the shifting ground they found themselves on prior to 1870.

The Civil War forced the United States to confront questions about who belonged in the nation and how that belonging could be proven. Often, military records were one of the places where those questions could be answered. By looking at how both the army and the navy classified and documented soldiers, historians can see how ideas about race and citizenship were not only debated on battlefields, but were recorded in the paperwork of the state.
